# How SSDs Work

The Solid State Drive (SSD) has quietly replaced the mechanical hard drive as the
default way computers store data. Boot times dropped from a minute to a few
seconds, laptops got thinner and quieter, and the familiar hum and click of a
spinning disk disappeared. But what actually changed inside the box?

This exhibit walks through **how an SSD works** — how it differs from a mechanical
Hard Disk Drive (HDD), how data is written and stored inside NAND flash memory, and
the role of the SSD controller that ties it all together.

## SSD vs. HDD: No Moving Parts

A traditional **HDD** stores data as magnetic charges on spinning platters. To read
or write, it swings a mechanical arm — the read/write head — to the right track and
waits for the platter to rotate the data underneath it. That physical movement is
tiny, but it adds up: every scattered piece of data means another *seek* and another
wait for the platter to spin into place.

An **SSD** has **no moving parts at all**. Data lives in flash memory chips, and the
drive reaches any location electronically. There is no head to move and no platter to
wait for, so the delay that dominates HDD performance simply disappears.

## Inside the SSD: NAND Flash Memory

The data on an SSD is stored in **NAND flash memory** — a type of *non-volatile*
memory, meaning it keeps its contents even when the power is off. That is what makes
it usable as permanent storage rather than temporary memory like RAM.

NAND flash is built from billions of tiny **memory cells**, each of which traps
electric charge to represent bits:

- **SLC** (Single-Level Cell) stores 1 bit per cell — fastest and most durable.
- **MLC / TLC / QLC** store 2, 3, or 4 bits per cell — cheaper and denser, but
  slower and with fewer write cycles before they wear out.

Cells are organized into a strict hierarchy: cells make up **pages** (the smallest
unit you can write), and pages are grouped into **blocks** (the smallest unit you can
erase).

### The catch: writing vs. erasing

Here is the quirk that shapes almost everything about SSD design:

> You can **write** data one page at a time, but you can only **erase** a whole
> **block** at once.

Because you cannot simply overwrite a single page in place, an SSD has to be clever
about *where* it puts data. When you "change" a file, the drive usually writes the
new version to a fresh page and marks the old one as stale. Later, a background
process called **garbage collection** consolidates the still-valid pages and erases
whole blocks to reclaim space. The **TRIM** command helps by letting the operating
system tell the drive which pages are no longer needed.

## The SSD Controller: The Brain of the Drive

None of this happens by magic — it is managed by the **SSD controller**, a small
processor built into every SSD. The controller is what makes a pile of flash chips
behave like a fast, reliable disk. Its main jobs:

- **Placement & mapping** — deciding which physical flash pages your data goes to,
  and keeping a map between the addresses the computer uses and the real locations.
- **Wear-leveling** — flash cells wear out after many write cycles, so the controller
  spreads writes evenly across all cells instead of hammering the same few. This is a
  major reason modern SSDs last for years of normal use.
- **Error correction (ECC)** — detecting and fixing the occasional bit that flips, so
  your data stays intact.
- **Garbage collection & TRIM** — reclaiming stale pages in the background to keep the
  drive fast as it fills up.
- **Talking to the computer** — communicating over the drive's interface.

## Interfaces: How Fast Can It Talk?

An SSD is only as fast as the connection it uses to reach the rest of the computer:

- **SATA** — the same interface older hard drives used. Convenient and compatible,
  but it caps out around **550 MB/s** — a bottleneck for modern flash.
- **NVMe over PCIe** — connects the SSD directly to the CPU's PCI Express lanes,
  bypassing the SATA bottleneck entirely. NVMe drives routinely reach **several
  thousand MB/s**, many times faster than SATA.

This is why two SSDs can feel very different: a NVMe drive and a SATA drive may use
similar flash, but the interface changes everything.
